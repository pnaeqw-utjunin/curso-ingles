Orador 1: Bienvenidos a un nuevo episodio de "American English File 2 Podcast". Les habla Emily.

Orador 2: Y yo soy Carlos. Hoy vamos a hablar sobre el tema "Lost Weekend", un fin de semana perdido. Vamos a explorar vocabulario, una historia interesante, gramática, pronunciación y más. ¡Comencemos!

Orador 1: Empezamos con una historia realista sobre un fin de semana fuera de lo común. A continuación escucharemos la historia de Steve. Presten atención para responder algunas preguntas sobre su experiencia.

Host: Last Friday Steve, a businessman from Seattle, was looking forward to a relaxing two days in the mountains. He and his wife had arranged a skiing weekend in a luxury hotel. But the weekend didn't work out exactly as they had planned. Steve worked until late on Friday evening. His office was on the 12th floor. When he finished at 8 o'clock, he locked his office and got into the elevator... and he didn't get out again until Monday morning!

Steve: I pressed the button for the first floor, and the elevator started going down, but then it stopped. I pressed the button again, but nothing happened. I pressed the alarm and shouted, but nobody heard me. Most people had already gone home. I tried to call my wife, but my cell phone didn't work in the elevator. I couldn't do anything. I just sat on the floor and hoped maybe somebody would realize what had happened. But on Saturday and Sunday, I knew nobody would be there. I slept most of the time to forget how hungry I was.

Host: Meanwhile Steve's wife, Kate, was waiting for her husband to come home.

Kate: I was very worried when he didn't come home on Friday evening, and I couldn't understand why his cell phone wasn't working. I called the police, and they looked for him, but they couldn't find him anywhere. I thought maybe he was with another woman.

Host: So Steve was in the elevator the whole weekend from Friday evening until Monday morning. At eight o'clock, when the office workers arrived, they called the emergency number, and somebody came and repaired the elevator.

Steve: I was very happy to get out. I hadn't eaten since Friday afternoon, and I was very hungry. It's lucky that I am not claustrophobic because the elevator was very small. The first thing I did was to call my wife to say that I was OK.

Host: Steve will soon be the fittest man in his office - from now on he's going to take the stairs every day - even though it's 12 floors.

Orador 2: Ahora vamos con algunas preguntas para comprobar tu comprensión:
1. What does Steve do? - ¿A qué se dedica Steve?
2. What floor was his office on? - ¿En qué piso estaba su oficina?
3. What happened when he first pressed the elevator button? - ¿Qué pasó cuando presionó el botón del ascensor por primera vez?
4. How did he try to get help? - ¿Cómo intentó pedir ayuda?
5. Where did Steve’s wife think he was? - ¿Dónde pensó su esposa que estaba Steve?
6. How did Steve get out of the elevator on Monday morning? - ¿Cómo salió del ascensor el lunes por la mañana?
7. How did he feel? - ¿Cómo se sintió?
8. What is Steve going to do every day now? - ¿Qué va a hacer Steve todos los días a partir de ahora?

Orador 1: También reflexionemos: ¿alguna vez te ha pasado algo similar? ¿Has estado atrapado o perdido sin que nadie lo sepa?

Orador 2: Muy bien. Ahora pasamos a una lectura muy interesante. A continuación leeremos la lectura: "What did you REALLY do on the weekend?"

WHAT DID YOU REALLY DO ON THE WEEKEND?
The next time a friend or co-worker tells you about their amazing weekend, wait a minute before you start feeling jealous - maybe they are inventing it all!

A survey of 5,000 adults has shown that one person in four invents details about their weekend because they want to impress their friends. When they are asked, "Did you have a good weekend?" they don't like to say that they just stayed at home and watched TV because it sounds boring. So they invent the details. The most common lie that people told was "I went out on Saturday night," when really they didn't go anywhere. Other common lies were "I had a romantic dinner," "I went to a party," and "I went away for the weekend."

In fact, in the survey, 30% of people who answered the questions said that they spent their weekend sleeping or resting because they were so tired at the end of the week.

Another 30% said that they needed to work or study on the weekend. Psychologist Corinne Sweet says that people often don't tell the truth about their weekend "because we don't want to feel that everyone else is having a better time than us, if we have had a boring weekend doing housework, paperwork, or just resting after a tiring week at work." She also believes that networking sites such as Facebook and Twitter may be encouraging us to invent details about our social lives. "People can create an illusion of who they want to be and the life they want to live," says Corinne, "and of course they want that life to seem exciting."

Orador 1: Esta lectura nos habla de cómo muchas personas exageran o inventan lo que hicieron el fin de semana para parecer más interesantes. Por ejemplo, en lugar de decir "I stayed at home and watched TV" - Me quedé en casa y vi televisión, dicen "I went to a party" - Fui a una fiesta.

Orador 2: Y según el texto, un 30% de las personas dijeron que trabajaron o estudiaron durante el fin de semana. Otro 30% dijo que necesitaba trabajar, pero en realidad no lo hicieron. Es interesante ver cómo la percepción social afecta lo que contamos.

Orador 1: Ahora pasemos a la gramática. Hoy practicamos el uso de las palabras: something (algo), anything (algo / nada), nothing (nada), etc.

Orador 2: Por ejemplo:
* I pressed the button again, but nothing happened. - Presioné el botón de nuevo, pero no pasó nada.
* The police couldn't find him anywhere. - La policía no pudo encontrarlo en ninguna parte.
* They called the emergency number and someone came. - Llamaron al número de emergencia y alguien vino.

Orador 1: Usemos estas palabras con lugares, cosas y personas:
* I want to go somewhere exciting. - Quiero ir a algún lugar emocionante.
* Did you buy anything? - ¿Compraste algo?
* Nobody was at the office. - No había nadie en la oficina.

Orador 2: Continuamos con la pronunciación de tres sonidos: /eə/, /əʊ/, y /aɪ/.

Orador 1: Escuchemos algunos ejemplos:
* /eə/ como en "egg" - huevo.
* /əʊ/ como en "phone" - teléfono.
* /aɪ/ como en "up" - arriba.

Orador 2: Practiquemos algunas frases con estos sonidos:
* Somebody's coming to lunch. - Alguien viene a almorzar.
* I never said anything. - Nunca dije nada.
* I've done nothing since Sunday. - No he hecho nada desde el domingo.

Orador 1: Vamos ahora a hablar sobre vocabulario, especialmente adjetivos que terminan en -ed y -ing. Estos adjetivos describen emociones o características.

Orador 2: Por ejemplo:
* tired (cansado) vs tiring (cansador)
* bored (aburrido) vs boring (aburrido que aburre)

Orador 1: Algunos ejemplos más:
* I was bored at the meeting. - Estaba aburrido en la reunión.
* The meeting was boring. - La reunión fue aburrida.
* That movie was depressing. - Esa película fue deprimente.
* I felt depressed. - Me sentí deprimido.

Orador 2: Ahora pasemos a la parte de speaking. Vamos a practicar con preguntas sobre el fin de semana.

Orador 1: Por ejemplo:
* Did you go anywhere exciting on Friday night? - ¿Fuiste a algún lugar emocionante el viernes por la noche?
* Did you clean or cook on Saturday morning? - ¿Limpiaste o cocinaste el sábado por la mañana?
* Did you study or work? - ¿Estudiaste o trabajaste?

Orador 2: Estas preguntas nos ayudan a practicar la forma pasada de los verbos y vocabulario cotidiano.

Orador 1: Para finalizar, vamos con una canción: "If You Love Somebody Set Them Free". Escuchar canciones en inglés es una excelente manera de mejorar la comprensión y el vocabulario.
