Ahora pasemos a la gramática. Hoy vamos a estudiar present perfect and simple past – presente perfecto y pasado simple. Estos dos tiempos verbales confunden mucho a los estudiantes, pero son muy importantes para hablar correctamente sobre experiencias y acciones pasadas.

Exacto. Empecemos con el present perfect – presente perfecto. La estructura es subject + have/has + past participle. Por ejemplo,
  • I have bought a new car – He comprado un coche nuevo.
  • She has saved a lot of money – Ella ha ahorrado mucho dinero.

Usamos el presente perfecto para acciones que ocurrieron en el pasado pero tienen una conexión con el presente, o cuando no especificamos exactamente cuándo ocurrió la acción. También para experiencias de vida sin mencionar el tiempo específico.

Por ejemplo,
  • Have you ever bought something online? – ¿Alguna vez has comprado algo en línea? Aquí no importa cuándo exactamente, solo si tienes esa experiencia o no.
  • I have lost my wallet – He perdido mi cartera. Mi cartera todavía está perdida, hay una conexión con el presente.

Más ejemplos.
  • She has been to Paris – Ella ha estado en París. Es una experiencia de vida.
  • They have saved five thousand dollars – Han ahorrado cinco mil dólares. El dinero probablemente todavía está ahorrado.

  • We have never wasted money on lottery tickets – Nunca hemos desperdiciado dinero en boletos de lotería.
  • He has worked very hard to be successful – Él ha trabajado muy duro para ser exitoso.
  • I have already paid the bill – Ya he pagado la cuenta.

Para hacer preguntas con presente perfecto, ponemos have/has antes del sujeto.
  • Have you paid the phone bill yet? – ¿Ya has pagado la cuenta del teléfono?
  • Has she ever won any money? – ¿Ella ha ganado dinero alguna vez?
  • How much money have you saved? – ¿Cuánto dinero has ahorrado?

Y para negativo, agregamos not después de have/has.
  • I haven't seen those shoes before – No he visto esos zapatos antes.
  • She hasn't spent all her money – Ella no ha gastado todo su dinero.
  • They haven't been to that store – No han ido a esa tienda.

Es importante conocer palabras que usamos frecuentemente con presente perfecto.

  • Just – justo o recién

  • Lately – últimamente

Por ejemplo,

  • I have just received my salary – Acabo de recibir mi salario.
  • We have recently opened a savings account – Recientemente abrimos una cuenta de ahorros.

Ahora hablemos del simple past – pasado simple. La estructura es subject + past form of verb. Para verbos regulares, agregamos -ed. Para verbos irregulares, usamos la forma específica del pasado.

Usamos el pasado simple para acciones completadas en el pasado en un tiempo específico. Es importante que el tiempo esté mencionado o esté claro en el contexto.
  • I bought a car last week – Compré un coche la semana pasada.
  • She saved money last year – Ella ahorró dinero el año pasado.

Más ejemplos.
  • I went shopping yesterday – Fui de compras ayer.
  • They paid the bill last month – Pagaron la cuenta el mes pasado.
  • He earned a million dollars in two thousand ten – Ganó un millón de dólares en dos mil diez.

  • We borrowed money from the bank three years ago – Pedimos dinero prestado al banco hace tres años.
  • She spent all her savings in two thousand twenty – Gastó todos sus ahorros en dos mil veinte.
  • I lost my credit card last Tuesday – Perdí mi tarjeta de crédito el martes pasado.

Para preguntas en pasado simple, usamos did antes del sujeto y el verbo en forma base.
  • Did you buy those shoes? – ¿Compraste esos zapatos?
  • Did she save any money? – ¿Ahorró ella algo de dinero?
  • When did they pay the mortgage? – ¿Cuándo pagaron la hipoteca?

Y para negativo, usamos didn't más el verbo en forma base.
  • I didn't spend much money – No gasté mucho dinero.
  • She didn't lend him any money – Ella no le prestó dinero.
  • They didn't afford to buy a house – No pudieron pagar una casa.

Ahora, la gran pregunta. ¿Cuál es la diferencia entre presente perfecto y pasado simple? La clave está en si mencionamos un tiempo específico o no, y si hay conexión con el presente.

Exacto. Comparemos.
  • I have bought a new phone – He comprado un teléfono nuevo. No menciono cuándo, y probablemente todavía lo tengo.
  • I bought a new phone yesterday – Compré un teléfono nuevo ayer. Tiempo específico, uso pasado simple.

Otro ejemplo.
  • She has saved a lot of money – Ella ha ahorrado mucho dinero. Probablemente todavía tiene ese dinero, conexión con el presente.
  • She saved a lot of money last year – Ella ahorró mucho dinero el año pasado. Acción completada en tiempo específico.

  • Have you ever lost your wallet? – ¿Alguna vez has perdido tu cartera? Experiencia de vida, no importa cuándo.
  • Did you lose your wallet last week? – ¿Perdiste tu cartera la semana pasada? Tiempo específico.

  • I have lived in this city for ten years – He vivido en esta ciudad por diez años. Empezó en el pasado pero continúa ahora, presente perfecto.
  • I lived in that city for ten years – Viví en esa ciudad por diez años. Ya no vivo ahí, acción completada, pasado simple.
Perfecto. También es importante mencionar que con palabras como yesterday – ayer, last week – la semana pasada, last year – el año pasado, in two thousand twenty – en dos mil veinte, ago – hace, siempre usamos pasado simple, nunca presente perfecto.

Y con palabras como ever – alguna vez, never – nunca, already – ya, yet – todavía, just – justo, recently – recientemente, generalmente usamos presente perfecto, especialmente cuando no especificamos el tiempo exacto.

From the streets to success! John DeJoria, an American billionaire businessman, owns several companies, including John Paul Mitchell Systems, a successful brand of hair products. However, DeJoria was not always wealthy. He was the second son of immigrant parents and grew up in a very poor area of Los Angeles, California. Before forming his hair product company with only seven hundred dollars, he was a street gang member for some of his youth. He then worked at low-paying jobs including encyclopedia salesman, janitor, and insurance salesman, and he was homeless twice. DeJoria's self-made rise is an inspiring story.

As a child, you were fairly entrepreneurial, weren't you? My first job, at nine years old, was selling Christmas cards door-to-door. At ten years old, my brother and I had paper routes. We got up at four o'clock in the morning, folded the papers, and delivered them, and then got ready for school.

As you got older, you continued to work. Is that right? The job I'm one of the most proud of perhaps was you can imagine was door-to-door selling encyclopedias. Doors literally slam in your face, maybe thirty, forty doors before the first customer will actually talk to you and let you in.

You've been homeless, haven't you? Twice. Once, when I was about twenty-two years old. The other time was when I started John Paul Mitchell Systems in nineteen eighty. I was getting along with my wife at the time. So I had left and had given her all the money. We had a bucket for John Paul Mitchell Systems putting in a half-million dollars. That money was supposed to arrive that day. So I just sort of slept in my car. And I slept in my car for the first two weeks when I started the company. So that was very humble beginnings.

Do the memories of the streets motivate you? It sure makes me very appreciative of everything I have in your life. Those who are homeless, like people with kids who are homeless, I really have a heart for. So I participate in a lot of charitable organizations that take care of the homeless of the streets.

What are the biggest problems you've had with business? The biggest problem is rejection. Any business you start, be ready for it. The difference is that successful people and unsuccessful people is that the successful people do all the things the unsuccessful people don't want to do. When ten doors are slammed in your face, go to door number eleven, with a smile.

When did you know you had made it? I was in business two years, and we were able to pay every single bill on time. We had a couple of thousand dollars in the bank, four thousand dollars, to be exact. And we said, man we made it, it's all downhill from here. It took a couple years.

What was the first thing you bought yourself at that point? I went to a restaurant. This is the first time I said I'm going to order off the left side of the menu, not the right side. The right side is the price side and the left side is the food. So I had filet mignon, I had lobster, I had an appetizer, I had salad, guacamole, whatever I wanted. I didn't even look at the prices. That, to me, was a pretty big deal.
