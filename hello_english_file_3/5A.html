Ahora pasemos a una historia fascinante sobre supersticiones deportivas. Esta lectura nos ayudará a entender mejor este fenómeno en el mundo del deporte. Escuchen con atención.


La historia se titula If I bounce the ball five times. Tennis players are strange people. Have you ever watched them at Wimbledon or the French Open? Before they serve, they always bounce the ball the same number of times. They bounce the ball instead of two, five times before serving, as if any change from their routine might result in disaster.

Matthew Syed, a famous British table tennis player, writes about sports superstitions in his fascinating book. One story involves Goran Ivanisevic, the Wimbledon champion in two thousand one. He was convinced that if he won a match, he had to repeat everything exactly the same way the next day. This meant eating at the same restaurant, talking to the same people, and even watching the same TV shows.

One year this meant that he had to watch Teletubbies every morning during his Wimbledon campaign. The children's show became part of his ritual. Sometimes it got very boring, he said, but he couldn't stop. It was part of his winning formula.

Another incredible story comes from tennis player Andy Murray. During the Wimbledon tournament, he was playing the fourth set when his wife suddenly got up and went to the kitchen. She was watching from home. He was losing games when she was in the room, she said. So if I go out now, he will win. And incredibly, he did win.

These superstitions aren't just limited to tennis. Last year, a survey of British soccer supporters found that twenty-one percent had a lucky charm. This could be anything from a scarf to a lucky coin. Another survey revealed that seventy percent of Spanish soccer fans performed pre-match rituals like wearing the same food or drink, or watching games with the same people.

But why do athletes develop these superstitions? Sports psychologists explain that in high-pressure situations, rituals give players a sense of control. When you're about to serve for the championship, bouncing the ball five times can calm your nerves. It's a way to focus and block out distractions.

Interestingly, superstitions aren't confined to the court or field. Female tennis players have their own unique rituals. When she was asked why she served so badly at the French Open, one player answered that she didn't tie her shoe laces right, and she didn't bounce the ball five times, so she just knew it wasn't going to happen. She believed her fate was sealed before she even hit the ball.

The most famous sports superstition might be the idea of a lucky streak. Athletes believe that success breeds success. If they're winning, they don't want to change anything that might break the spell. This explains why some players refuse to wash their uniforms during a winning streak or insist on entering the stadium through the same door.

In basketball, some players have elaborate pre-game routines that can last thirty minutes or more. They might shoot from specific spots on the court in a precise order, wear their gear in a particular sequence, or listen to the same playlist before every game. These rituals become deeply ingrained.

Even coaches get involved in superstitions. Some wear the same jacket throughout a playoff series, others stand in the exact same spot on the sideline, and many have specific phrases they repeat before games. It's not just the athletes who believe in these rituals.

What's particularly interesting is how these superstitions spread among teammates. If one player starts a ritual and the team wins, others might adopt similar behaviors. It becomes a team culture, a shared belief system that can strengthen bonds between players.

Of course, not everyone believes in superstitions. Some athletes are completely rational and dismiss these rituals as nonsense. They argue that skill, preparation, and mental toughness are what really matter, not whether you bounce the ball three times or five times.

But even skeptics admit that if a ritual helps an athlete feel more confident and focused, then it's serving a purpose. The mind is incredibly powerful in sports, and anything that helps a player perform at their best has value, whether it's scientifically proven or not.

So next time you watch a tennis match or a soccer game, pay attention to the little rituals players perform. You might notice someone bouncing a ball repeatedly, touching their socks in a specific way, or looking up at the sky before serving. These aren't random actions—they're carefully crafted superstitions that give athletes a sense of control in unpredictable situations.

Ahora pasemos a la gramática, donde nos enfocaremos en los tiempos pasados: simple past, past continuous y past perfect. Estos tiempos son esenciales para contar historias y hablar sobre eventos deportivos que ya ocurrieron.

Comencemos con el simple past – pasado simple. Lo usamos para acciones completadas en un momento específico del pasado. Por ejemplo, Goran Ivanisevic won Wimbledon in two thousand one – Goran Ivanisevic ganó Wimbledon en dos mil uno. La acción está terminada y sabemos cuándo ocurrió.

Otros ejemplos:
  • She bounced the ball five times – Ella rebotó la pelota cinco veces
  • They played tennis yesterday – Ellos jugaron tenis ayer
  • The referee made a mistake – El árbitro cometió un error

Ahora el past continuous – pasado continuo. Lo usamos para acciones que estaban en progreso en un momento específico del pasado. La estructura es was o were más el verbo con -ing. Por ejemplo, Andy Murray was playing the fourth set when his wife left the room – Andy Murray estaba jugando el cuarto set cuando su esposa salió de la habitación.

Nota que usamos past continuous para la acción larga que estaba en progreso, was playing, y simple past para la acción corta que la interrumpió, left. Más ejemplos:
  • The spectators were watching the race when the runner collapsed – Los espectadores estaban viendo la carrera cuando el corredor colapsó

Otro ejemplo:
  • While she was serving, it started to rain – Mientras ella estaba sacando, comenzó a llover
  • While I was training, my coach called me – Mientras yo estaba entrenando, mi entrenador me llamó

El past continuous establece el contexto, la escena de fondo.

Ahora el past perfect – pasado perfecto. Este tiempo se usa para una acción que ocurrió antes de otra acción en el pasado. La estructura es had más el participio pasado del verbo. Por ejemplo, The organizers were surprised because she had not been sweating very much – Los organizadores estaban sorprendidos porque ella no había estado sudando mucho.

Aquí vemos que el no sudar ocurrió antes de que los organizadores se sorprendieran. El past perfect nos ayuda a establecer el orden de los eventos. Otro ejemplo de nuestra lectura: During the last half mile, Rosie suddenly jumped out of the crowd because the marathon organizers had taken her title away – Durante la última media milla, Rosie saltó repentinamente de la multitud porque los organizadores del maratón le habían quitado su título.

Más ejemplos:
  • He realized that he had forgotten his lucky charm – Él se dio cuenta de que había olvidado su amuleto de la suerte
  • They discovered that she had cheated three months earlier – Ellos descubrieron que ella había hecho trampa tres meses antes
  • Rosie had taken the subway before she finished the race – Rosie había tomado el metro antes de terminar la carrera

Cuando combinamos los tres tiempos, podemos contar historias complejas. Escucha este ejemplo:
  • While the team was celebrating their victory, someone revealed that one player had used performance-enhancing drugs during the season – Mientras el equipo estaba celebrando su victoria, alguien reveló que un jugador había usado drogas para mejorar el rendimiento durante la temporada

Aquí tenemos past continuous para was celebrating, simple past para revealed, y past perfect para had used. Cada tiempo tiene su función específica. El past continuous describe la escena, el simple past introduce la nueva información, y el past perfect explica qué había pasado antes.

Practiquemos con más ejemplos combinados.
  • The referee hadn't seen the foul because he was looking at another player – El árbitro no había visto la falta porque estaba mirando a otro jugador
  • When the coach arrived, the team had already started warming up – Cuando el entrenador llegó, el equipo ya había comenzado a calentar

Otro ejemplo importante:
  • She couldn't play because she had injured her ankle while she was training the day before – Ella no pudo jugar porque se había lesionado el tobillo mientras estaba entrenando el día anterior

Aquí usamos los tres tiempos para crear una narrativa clara.

Una regla importante: cuando tenemos dos acciones en el pasado y queremos enfatizar que una ocurrió antes que la otra, usamos past perfect para la primera acción y simple past para la segunda. Por ejemplo:
  • After he had won the championship, he retired from professional sports – Después de que había ganado el campeonato, se retiró de los deportes profesionales

Ahora la última sección, writing. Aquí les propongo que escriban una historia sobre algo que les sucedió relacionado con deportes. Puede ser un momento divertido, emocionante, o incluso embarazoso.

La estructura debe incluir el contexto usando past continuous, los eventos principales usando simple past, y si necesitan explicar algo que ocurrió antes, usen past perfect. Por ejemplo:
  • Last summer, I was training for a marathon when something funny happened – El verano pasado, estaba entrenando para un maratón cuando algo gracioso sucedió

Continúa la historia:
  • I had been running for about thirty minutes when I realized I had forgotten my water bottle at home – Había estado corriendo durante unos treinta minutos cuando me di cuenta de que había olvidado mi botella de agua en casa

Y el final:
  • While I was looking for a water fountain, I ran into my old coach who was also training – Mientras buscaba una fuente de agua, me encontré con mi antiguo entrenador que también estaba entrenando

Noten cómo usamos los tres tiempos pasados para crear una narrativa fluida.

Otro tema podría ser escribir sobre una superstición deportiva que tienen o que han observado. Por ejemplo:
  • My brother has always believed that wearing red socks brings him good luck in soccer matches – Mi hermano siempre ha creído que usar calcetines rojos le trae buena suerte en los partidos de fútbol

O pueden escribir sobre un evento deportivo memorable:
  • The most exciting match I ever watched was when my local team was losing three to zero at halftime, but they had scored four goals in the second half and won the game – El partido más emocionante que vi fue cuando mi equipo local estaba perdiendo tres a cero en el medio tiempo, pero habían anotado cuatro goles en el segundo tiempo y ganaron el partido

Recuerden incluir detalles específicos: qué estaban haciendo, qué pasó, cómo se sintieron, y qué había ocurrido antes que explique el contexto. Esto hace que sus historias sean más interesantes y les permite practicar todos los tiempos verbales.
